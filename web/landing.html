Under the hood

A real rules engine, not just a chatty narrator

The language model tells the story. Code — not the model — rolls the dice, tracks HP, and resolves checks, so the numbers are exact every time.

Real dice, engine-owned HP & checks

Rolls, HP, conditions, and DC-resolved ability checks are computed by deterministic code (/dm damage, /dm heal, /dm check STR 15) — the model narrates the outcome, it doesn't invent it.

Persistent campaign memory

A rolling "living summary" plus per-turn recall means your DM remembers what happened ten sessions ago — without you replaying the whole log every time you sit down.

Multiplayer, wherever your group talks

Discord, Slack, Matrix, Mattermost, or the browser — one party, one campaign, shared dice and a shared battle map, with private fog-of-war whispers only the target player sees.

Model-agnostic — bring your own key

OpenAI-compatible providers, native Anthropic, a local Ollama model, or a free OpenRouter key. Switch models mid-campaign; your key stays on your device by default and is sent only to the endpoint you chose.

A real desktop app, same engine

Electron (and a lighter Tauri build) wraps the identical table UI and rules engine so solo or hotseat play works fully offline, with no server required — the same code that runs the browser client.

Character creator & portraits

Twelve classic classes, a procedural heraldic portrait for every character (or your own art / an imported character card), visible to the whole party on a shared token board.

How it works

No account, no signup — just a model and a place to talk.

1

Bring a model

Paste a free OpenRouter key, point at a local Ollama model, or use your own OpenAI/Anthropic key. It's yours — never stored on a server.

2

Open your table

Invite the bot to Discord, Slack, Matrix, or Mattermost — or just open the browser or desktop app, solo or with friends.

3

Say /dm new

Then just describe what you do in plain language, like you would at a real table.

4

The engine resolves it

Dice, HP, and DC checks are computed by code, not guessed by the model — the AI narrates whatever actually happened.
